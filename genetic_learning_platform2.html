Genetic Conditions: Clinical Reasoning Platform

Objective: Given family history and presentation, apply genetic risk considerations to select targeted screening questions and determine appropriate referral triggers.

Case 1

A 12-year-old presents with frequent fractures, blue sclera, and reports that their mother had similar issues growing up.

Osteogenesis Imperfecta
Findings: Fragile bones, scoliosis
Interventions: Low-load strengthening, education
Precautions: Avoid high impact

Case 2

A 45-year-old presents with progressive chorea, cognitive decline, and a father with similar symptoms.

Huntington Disease
Findings: Chorea, balance deficits
Interventions: Balance training, safety
Precautions: Fall risk

Case 3

A 6-year-old boy presents with frequent falls, Gowers' sign, and calf pseudohypertrophy. Family history reveals an uncle with early wheelchair use.

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy
Findings: Progressive weakness
Interventions: Stretching, function preservation
Precautions: Avoid overfatigue

Case 4

A 25-year-old presents with joint hypermobility, frequent dislocations, and skin elasticity.

Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome
Findings: Hypermobility, instability
Interventions: Stability training
Precautions: Avoid overstretching

Ethical Considerations (Faculty Guidance)

Genetic Testing Privacy:
Patient genetic data is highly sensitive. PTs must ensure compliance with HIPAA and avoid unnecessary sharing of genetic information. Only relevant clinical information should be documented and communicated.

Discrimination Risk:
Patients may face discrimination in employment or insurance based on genetic information. While laws like GINA provide protection, gaps still exist (e.g., life insurance). PTs should avoid labeling or documenting genetic risks in ways that could unintentionally harm patients.

Direct-to-Consumer Genetic Tests:
Results from commercial tests (e.g., 23andMe) may lack clinical validity or context. PTs should not make clinical decisions based solely on these results and should encourage confirmation through medical-grade testing when appropriate.

Clinical Decision-Making:
Genetic information should inform—but not dictate—care. PTs must integrate genetics with presentation, environment, and behavior.

Referral Triggers:
Refer to physicians or genetic counselors when:

  • Strong family history of progressive or unexplained conditions
  • Early onset of atypical impairments
  • Rapid functional decline without clear cause

Key Takeaway:
PTs should interpret genetic information cautiously, prioritize patient safety, and collaborate closely with physicians and genetic counselors to ensure appropriate management.
